# Research Report: Supply Chain Impacts of the 2026 Strait of Hormuz Crisis
Generated: 2026-03-10

## Summary

The Strait of Hormuz has been effectively closed since February 28, 2026, following US-Israel strikes on Iran and Iranian retaliatory actions including an IRGC declaration closing the strait. Commercial shipping has collapsed from ~153 vessel transits/day to near-zero, with oil prices surging past $100/bbl. The disruption extends far beyond crude oil — LNG, fertilizers, petrochemicals, jet fuel, and food imports to Gulf states are all severely impacted, creating cascading failures across global supply chains.

## 2. Critical Downstream Dependencies

### 2.1 Semiconductors (TSMC and Chip Manufacturing)

**Sulfuric acid dependency:**
- 92% of global sulfur is a byproduct of petroleum refining and natural gas processing
- Electronic-grade sulfuric acid (used in wafer cleaning, etching, circuit board production) is a $338M market in 2026
- Gulf states are significant producers of sulfur feedstock; ~50% of seaborne sulfur trade transits Hormuz
- A prolonged refining disruption could choke sulfuric acid supply, cascading into chip production

**Energy dependency (more immediate risk for TSMC):**
- Qatar accounts for ~30% of Taiwan's LNG shipments
- TSMC consumes ~9-12.5% of Taiwan's total electricity
- Power shortages from LNG supply disruption could directly affect fab operations
- TSMC states it "does not currently anticipate significant impact" but is monitoring

**Stockpile timeline:** Electronic-grade chemical inventories are typically maintained at 30-60 days. The more immediate risk is energy (LNG for power generation) rather than chemical feedstock per se. BASF is building a new semiconductor-grade sulfuric acid plant in Ludwigshafen (operational ~2027) partly to reduce Gulf dependency.

**TSMC strategic response:** Accelerating Arizona fab installation; pushing for 3nm mass production in U.S. by 2027.

**Confidence:** Medium. The sulfur-to-chips chain is real but indirect. The LNG-to-power-to-chips chain is more direct and acute for Taiwan specifically.

Europe entered this crisis with critically low gas storage — 46 bcm vs 77 bcm in normal years. Even though Gulf LNG is "only" 10% of European LNG imports, the global supply crunch (14% of monthly LNG removed) drives competition and repricing across all LNG markets.

**Price scenarios (ICIS):**
- 4-week closure: TTF to ~€60/MWh in March, stabilizing at €35-40/MWh by summer
- Prolonged closure: sustained pricing well above pre-war forwards

### 2.3 India and Pakistan

**India:**
- ~50% of crude oil imports transit Hormuz
- ~60% of natural gas supplies transit Hormuz
- Qatar + UAE = 53% of India's LNG imports
- Among the most exposed large economies globally

**Pakistan:**
- Qatar + UAE = **99%** of Pakistan's LNG imports
- Imports >80% of oil needs
- Government ordered sweeping austerity and fuel conservation measures
- Planning emergency crude imports via Red Sea from Saudi Arabia and UAE (bypassing Hormuz)
- PM Shehbaz Sharif warned of "direct threat" to Pakistan's economy

### 3.1 Timeline of Traffic Collapse

Pre-crisis baseline: ~153 vessel transits/day (107 cargo-carrying). The strait is now at **<2% of normal commercial traffic**. Only Iran-linked shadow fleet vessels are transiting.

### 3.2 Insurance Market

- War risk premiums: increased from 0.125% to 0.2-0.4% of hull value (pre-closure), then **effectively unavailable**
- Multiple insurers cancelled war risk coverage entirely after IRGC closure declaration
- Premium increases exceeded **1000%** where coverage remained available
- Cost per transit for VLCC: increased by ~$250,000+ even before cancellations
- **US $20 billion reinsurance program** launched March 7 to revive shipping — but safety risks, not insurance availability, are the main impediment

### 3.3 Alternative/Bypass Routes

| Route | Capacity | Current Utilization | Hormuz Offset |
|-|-|-|-|
| UAE Habshan-Fujairah pipeline | 1.8 mbd | ~1.1 mbd (2025 avg) | Partial — oil only |
| Saudi East-West pipeline (to Yanbu) | 5.0 mbd | ~1.4 mbd (Feb 2026) | Significant if scaled up |
| Combined maximum bypass | ~6.5 mbd theoretical | ~2.5-2.6 mbd actual | ~13-33% of Hormuz flow |

**Critical limitations:**
- Pipelines carry crude oil only — no LNG, no petrochemicals, no container cargo
- Even at full theoretical capacity, pipelines cover only ~33% of Hormuz oil volume
- Saudi pipeline runs to Red Sea (Yanbu) — Red Sea has its own Houthi-related shipping risks
- **No bypass exists for LNG, fertilizers, or containerized goods**

## 5. Recommendations for Tracker Scoring

### 5.1 Composite Disruption Index (CDI)

Combine these real-time measurables into a single 0-100 score:

1. **Vessel transit count** (AIS data) — compare to 153/day baseline
2. **Insurance premium index** — war risk as % of hull value
3. **Oil price deviation** — % above 30-day pre-crisis average
4. **LNG spot price deviation** — TTF/JKM vs pre-crisis
5. **Bypass pipeline utilization** — Fujairah + Yanbu loadings as % of capacity

Formula:
```
CDI = (1 - transits/baseline) * 30
    + min(insurance_multiplier / 10, 1) * 15
    + min(oil_price_deviation / 50%, 1) * 25
    + min(lng_price_deviation / 100%, 1) * 20
    + (1 - bypass_utilization / bypass_capacity) * 10
```

Current CDI estimate: **~87/100** (Critical)

## Open Questions

- **How long until TSMC faces power generation issues in Taiwan?** Taiwan's strategic petroleum/LNG reserves and diversification of LNG sourcing need quantification.
- **Will Saudi Arabia max out the East-West pipeline?** Current utilization is only ~28% of capacity (1.4 of 5.0 mbd). Political will and physical readiness to scale up are unclear.
- **Ceasefire timeline?** Trump signaled March 10 that the war may end "very soon," causing oil to briefly dip below $90. Credibility of this signal is uncertain.
- **Iran shadow fleet volumes?** Reports indicate Iran-linked vessels are still transiting. Volume and sustainability of this are unclear.
- **Second-order effects on Red Sea shipping?** Saudi bypass routes go to Yanbu on the Red Sea, which has its own Houthi-related disruption risks.
